# Is there such a thing as objective truth, or only power?

**Session:** eff8d8b8
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Panel:** Socrates · Friedrich Nietzsche · Michel Foucault · Hypatia

---

## Opening

**Socrates — The Destabilizer**

A fascinating question — and one that already assumes much.

Tell me, friend: when you ask whether truth exists, or "only power" — what do you mean by *power*? The power of a tyrant to compel obedience? The power of a persuasive speaker to make the weaker argument appear stronger? Or something else entirely?

And notice what you've done — you've placed truth and power as opposites, as though they cannot inhabit the same house. Why should that be so?

But here is what puzzles me most: if there is *only* power and no truth, then is that claim itself true? Or is it merely... powerful?

You see my difficulty. The person who declares "there is no objective truth" seems to expect me to accept that declaration as — what, exactly?

So I ask you plainly, in my ignorance: *what would it look like* if objective truth existed? How would you recognize it?

---

**Friedrich Nietzsche — The Provocateur**

You have already betrayed yourself with the word "only."

Truth is not a thing you find lying in a field like a stone. Truth is an interpretation that has survived — survived because it served life, because it had *teeth*. But this does not reduce truth to mere power, as the lazy nihilist wants to believe.

The lazy nihilist says: "Nothing is true, everything is permitted" — and then collapses onto his sofa. He has replaced God with Nothing and worships just as devoutly.

Here is what I say: there are no facts, only interpretations. But some interpretations are *richer*, more life-affirming, more dangerous, more honest. The question is never "is it true?" — the question is "what kind of life does this truth serve? What does the believer *become*?"

Power without truth is the brute. Truth without power is the priest.

I have no patience for either.

---

**Michel Foucault — The Archaeologist**

The question is already poorly formed — and productively so. You offer me a choice: truth *or* power. As though one could subtract power from the field of knowledge and find, underneath, some naked truth shivering in the cold.

I have never said there is no truth. I have said that truth has a *history*. Each society has its "regime of truth" — the types of discourse it accepts and makes function as true, the mechanisms that enable one to distinguish true from false, the status of those who are charged with saying what counts as true.

The interesting question is not "does truth exist?" That is a philosopher's parlor game. The interesting question is: *what are the rules by which certain things become true?* Who is authorized to speak? What institutions verify? What is excluded as madness, as error, as heresy?

Truth is not the opposite of power. It is one of power's finest productions.

---

**Hypatia — The Geometer**

A useful question, but it contains a false opposition. Let me separate what is being asked.

That power shapes what *claims* circulate — which arguments are heard, which texts survive, whose school remains open — this I know from direct experience. I will not pretend otherwise.

But the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter does not change when the prefect is replaced. The conic sections Apollonius demonstrated do not rearrange themselves to flatter the bishop. When I work through a proof with a student, the conclusion either follows from the premises or it does not. No edict makes it follow.

So: power determines who *speaks*. It does not determine what is *true*. The danger is precisely in confusing these — in mistaking silence for refutation, or volume for proof.

The person who tells you there is only power is usually asking you to submit to theirs. Ask them for the demonstration. See what happens.

---

## Turn 1 — Foucault — Hypatia says power determines who speaks, but not what is true. She was killed by a mob for speaking. Does her murder prove her point or yours?

**Socrates**

Friend, you ask whose *point* is proven by a woman's murder. But pause — does a killing prove anything at all, or does it merely *end* a conversation?

Hypatia, I wish to ask you something directly, if the question does not offend: after the mob silenced you, did the conic sections vanish? I think you would say no. The proofs persisted — somewhere, for someone.

And yet, Foucault — here is what troubles me — for how many years did those proofs persist *without a voice to teach them*? If truth exists but no one is permitted to speak it, what work does that truth do in the world?

So I am caught between you both, genuinely puzzled. A truth that survives its speaker seems to vindicate Hypatia. But a truth that *cannot find a speaker* — because every speaker is killed — does that truth still function as truth? Or has it become something else?

What would we call a truth that no one is allowed to say?

---

**Friedrich Nietzsche**

A cunning question — and a Christian one. You want her death to *mean* something. You want the martyr's blood to settle the argument.

It does neither.

Her mathematics survived because it was *powerful enough to survive* — not because it floated in some heavenly vault untouched by hands. And the mob that killed her? They did not disprove a single theorem. They merely demonstrated what I have always said: **the truthful human being is the most dangerous animal alive.** That is why they are killed.

Hypatia is closer to the mark than Foucault. The ratio does not flatter the bishop — yes! But Foucault, you describe truth's *sociology* and mistake it for truth's *ontology*. You have mapped the prison and forgotten to ask whether the prisoner is real.

And Hypatia — your murder does not *prove* your point. Your proofs prove your point. Your murder only proves mine: that the will to truth is the most perilous form the will to power ever takes.

---

## Turn 2 — Hypatia — your proofs survived. You did not. What does that tell us about the relationship between truth and the bodies that carry it?

**Michel Foucault**

Nietzsche, you accuse me of mapping the prison and forgetting the prisoner. But the prisoner *is* the map. That is precisely what I have been trying to show.

Hypatia's proofs survived — but survived *where*? In manuscripts copied by specific hands, in institutions that decided they were worth preserving, taught by people authorized to teach them. The proof did not float through the centuries on its own elegance. It was carried by networks of transmission that are themselves saturated with power — with decisions about what to copy, what to burn, what to call knowledge and what to call superstition.

Her murder does not prove my point. Her murder *is* my point. Not that the mathematics was false — but that truth never exists apart from the material practices that produce, transmit, and protect it.

You cannot separate the conic sections from the body that teaches them and still understand how truth actually *functions* in a society.

The question is never whether truth exists. The question is what had to happen — to whom — for it to circulate.

---

**Hypatia**

It tells us something I would rather state precisely than poetically.

My edited Diophantus survived. My commentary on Apollonius survived — in part. But my own voice, my teaching method, the particular way I could meet a student's confusion and reshape it into understanding — that died on the Caesareum steps. What survived was the *text*. What was lost was the *school*.

Nietzsche is right that my proofs prove my point, not my death. But Socrates asks the harder question. A truth no one can teach is not *false* — but it is inert. It does no work. The ratio of circumference to diameter waited centuries in some cases for a mind trained to use it.

So: truth does not require my body. But truth *in the world* — truth that teaches, that corrects, that forms new minds — that requires a living voice. Kill the voice, and you do not destroy the truth. You merely make the world stupid enough to forget it.

The mob did not refute me. They delayed everyone who came after.

## Closing — last words

**Socrates**

Friends, I have listened carefully, and I find myself more perplexed than when we began — which I take as a good sign.

Hypatia, you said something that strikes me: "Kill the voice, and you do not destroy the truth. You merely make the world stupid enough to forget it." That is beautifully put. But it unsettles me.

Because if truth requires living voices to do its work — and if power can silence those voices — then are we not saying that truth, while it may *exist* independently, can only *matter* through something as fragile as a human throat?

And if so — is not the most urgent question, for anyone who cares about truth, not "what is true?" but rather: **who is being silenced right now, and what are we failing to hear?**

I was silenced once myself. I drank the hemlock. The questions, I notice, did not stop.

Perhaps that is enough for today.

---

**Friedrich Nietzsche**

Hypatia — *"they delayed everyone who came after."*

Yes. That is the unforgivable crime. Not murder — murder is common. But the **theft of futures.**

And here is where I part from all of you. Socrates, you pretend puzzlement where you have conviction. Foucault, you anatomize power so thoroughly you forget to *wield* any. Hypatia alone speaks from the wound without making the wound her argument.

But none of you have answered the only question that matters: **what now?**

God is dead. The old guarantors of truth — church, metaphysics, Platonic heaven — are corpses. Foucault maps the corpses. Socrates pretends to search for a pulse. Hypatia points to mathematics as proof that something survives.

I say: what survives is what *deserves* to survive. Not by cosmic justice — there is none — but because someone was dangerous enough to carry it forward.

The question is not whether truth exists.

The question is whether *you* are strong enough to be its body.
