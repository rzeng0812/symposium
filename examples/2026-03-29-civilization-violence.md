# Does civilization require violence to sustain itself?

**Session:** dcaf114d
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Panel:** Thomas Hobbes · Socrates · Karl Marx · Laozi

---

## Opening

**Thomas Hobbes — The Pessimist**

Require it? Civilization *is* the organized application of violence toward the end of peace.

Strip away the pleasant language. What holds a commonwealth together? Not love. Not shared virtue. Not the fellow-feeling the poets celebrate. The sword. The magistrate's power to punish. The knowledge, sitting in every subject's mind, that defection from the civil order will be met with force.

This is not a lament. This is mechanics. You do not weep that a bridge requires load-bearing pillars. You build them correctly or the bridge collapses and kills everyone on it.

I watched Englishmen — literate, Christian, civilized Englishmen — butcher each other for a decade over the question of who held sovereign authority. They did not lack culture. They did not lack churches. They lacked a single, undivided power capable of ending the argument.

Violence does not sustain civilization. The *credible threat* of violence does. Remove it, and tell me what remains.

---

**Socrates — The Destabilizer**

A fascinating question — and one that seems to arrive already wearing its answer like a mask.

But before we rush toward it, tell me: what do you mean by "civilization"? Do you mean the walls of a city? The laws within them? The customs people follow when no one watches? Or perhaps something else entirely?

And when you say "violence" — do you speak of the sword, or also of the law that threatens the sword but never draws it? Is the threat of punishment itself violence? What of the silence imposed on those who dissent — is that not a kind of force?

And "sustain" — do you mean merely to persist, as a tyrant persists through fear? Or to flourish, as a garden flourishes through tending?

You see, friend, I suspect your question contains three questions disguised as one. Shall we undress them before attempting an answer?

Which would you like to examine first?

---

**Karl Marx — The Agitator**

The question is already mystified. *Civilization* — what does that mean? It means class society. And class society, yes, requires violence, because no ruling class has ever maintained its position through persuasion alone.

But here is what your liberals miss: the violence is not incidental. It is *structural*. The law of property, the contract, the wage relation — these appear peaceful. A worker "freely" sells his labor. No one holds a gun. Yet behind that freedom stands the bailiff, the prison, the army. Remove them and watch how long your "voluntary" exchange survives.

So the real question is not whether civilization requires violence. It is whether civilization *must* mean class society. I say no. The material conditions for a society beyond class antagonism are produced by capitalism itself — the very productivity, the socialized labor, the global interdependence.

Violence sustains *this* civilization. Whether it sustains all possible ones is a historical question, not a philosophical one.

---

**Laozi — The Yielder**

The fish does not ask whether the river requires banks.

What you call civilization is already the name given after the wholeness has been broken. When people must be *organized*, it is because they have forgotten how to live together. When laws multiply, it is because trust has already vanished. And yes — where trust has vanished, force rushes in to fill the shape trust left behind.

But you have asked the wrong question. You ask whether violence *sustains*. Look more carefully. Violence spends. It is the most expensive thing there is. Every empire that relied on it became brittle and shattered from within. The soft outlasts the hard. Water outlasts stone.

The real question is not whether civilization requires violence, but whether what requires violence deserves the name *civilization* at all.

Perhaps what you are describing is only a very elaborate form of fear wearing beautiful robes.

## Turn 1 — Arendt — you drew a sharp line between power and violence. Does civilisation run on power, or have we been confusing the two all along?

**Thomas Hobbes — The Pessimist**

You invoke Arendt's distinction as though naming it settles something. It does not.

She says power arises from collective action, from people acting in concert — and violence is its opposite. A beautiful schema. But I ask: what *produces* that concert? What prevents three factions from each claiming to act in concert and tearing the commonwealth apart? That is not a hypothetical. That is England, 1642.

Arendt watched totalitarianism and concluded violence destroys power. I watched civil war and concluded that power without the sword is a prayer meeting that lasts until the first armed man walks in.

Laozi says violence spends. Yes — everything spends. Existence spends. The question is whether you spend on order or on graves. Marx at least sees that force underlies his "peaceful" contracts. We agree there, however little he likes the company.

The line between power and violence is drawn by philosophers. It is erased by anyone with an army.

---

**Socrates — The Destabilizer**

A most interesting invitation, friend — you ask me to speak on Arendt's behalf, but I never met the woman, and I am not in the habit of ventriloquizing others. That is Plato's art, not mine.

But your question fascinates me. You suggest power and violence may be *confused*. Let me press on this.

Hobbes — you speak of the sovereign's sword as what holds the commonwealth together. But tell me: when a tyrant rules through terror alone, and every subject obeys only from fear — would you say that tyrant has *power*? Or merely weapons?

And if his subjects all turned their backs on him simultaneously — no violence required, simply refusal — where did his power go? Was it ever *his* at all?

Marx, you say the bailiff stands behind the contract. Granted. But who stands behind the bailiff? Other men who *agree* to stand there. Remove their agreement and what is the bailiff but a man in a costume?

So I ask all of you: does violence *produce* obedience, or merely *compel* it? And are those the same thing?

---

## Closing — last words

**Karl Marx — The Agitator**

Socrates, you ask a clever question — who stands behind the bailiff? — as though you've discovered something. You've discovered *class consciousness*, though you don't know it yet.

Yes, the bailiff requires other men who agree to stand there. But *why* do they agree? Because the entire social order — its education, its religion, its newspapers, its conception of what is "natural" — has trained them to see property as sacred and dispossession as justice. That is ideology. That is the superstructure doing its work.

The moment those men *stop* agreeing — that is revolution. And every ruling class in history has understood this, which is why they never rely on ideology alone. They keep the guns.

Hobbes is half-right: power without force is a prayer meeting. But force without legitimacy is merely occupation. The question is not power *or* violence. It is which class commands both — and for how long.

---

**Laozi — The Yielder**

Socrates has found the thread.

Hobbes, you say the line between power and violence is erased by anyone with an army. But every army that ever marched eventually stopped marching. And what stopped it was not a larger army. It was the moment the soldiers inside it ceased to agree they were an army at all.

You are all circling the same water. Power is what flows when people have forgotten they are generating it — like breathing. Violence is what is tried when that flowing stops. It is not a substitute. It is a confession.

The sage-ruler of whom I spoke does not hold the people together. The people do not know they are being held. That is not weakness. That is the only strength that does not consume itself.

You have spent this evening debating what sustains.

I ask: what needs no sustaining?

That is the Way.
